Core thesis: Managing an engineering team and building a basketball roster are the same problem. Most people optimize for talent accumulation. The real game is fit, timeline, and role clarity.

1. **Roster Construction: You Can't Just Stack Stars**
   - As sexy as it is to hire all top-tier talent, the pieces have to fit together.

   - If you have a generational talent, you don't make them adapt to your system. You build the system around them.
   - The Warriors built a motion-offense system around Curry's range. The Cavs cleared the floor for LeBron to drive.
   - Same in engineering. If you have someone who's exceptional at a specific thing, your job as a manager is to build the environment where that thing is maximally valuable.
   - Counterpoint: what if your "LeBron" doesn't want to be the system? What if they want to be a role player?

3. **The Timeline Problem**
   - In basketball, roster construction is time-bounded. You have your superstar's prime window.
   - Same with startups. You have runway, you have market timing, you have personal burnout timelines.
   - Managing incentives: the rookie who needs reps vs. the veteran who needs a ring.
   - How to align individual career goals with team timeline.

4. **The Mid-Level Exception (Role Players Are Load-Bearing)**
   - The 3-and-D wing who doesn't show up in box scores but makes the whole defense work.
   - The engineer who closes PRs reliably, writes clear docs, and makes everyone else better without being flashy.
   - Why optimizing for "10x engineers" is like optimizing for scorers — you end up with a team that can't defend.

5. **When to Blow It Up**
   - Every NBA GM faces the question: do we run it back or rebuild?
   - Same in management. Do you try to fix the team you have or start over?
   - The sunk cost of roster construction. The emotional cost of letting people go.
